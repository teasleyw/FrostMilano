THE VICTROLA

Every one of these records was cut into wax, pressed in shellac, and played on a spring you had to wind. It never sounded clean. That was never the point.

Why it hisses: Paramount, who pressed most of what's on this page, was a side business of a Wisconsin chair company. They mixed their shellac cheap and pressed it thin. That sandstorm behind Charley Patton isn't age — a lot of it was there the day the record was new.

THE LINEAGE

Three men. One rope. Turn a card over for the notes.

PARAMOUNT · CHICAGO BLIND LEMON
JEFFERSON c. 1893 – 1929 COUTCHMAN, TEXAS

THE TEXAS END OF THE ROPE

BLIND LEMON JEFFERSON

Born
About 1893, Coutchman, Freestone County, Texas. Blind from the start.
Worked
Street corners in Deep Ellum, Dallas — for a while alongside a young Huddie Ledbetter, later known as Lead Belly.
Recorded
Paramount, from 1926. Close to a hundred sides in four years, more than almost anyone of his generation.
Sides
Got the Blues · Black Snake Moan · Match Box Blues · See That My Grave Is Kept Clean
Died
Chicago, December 1929. The story is a heart attack in the snow; nobody agrees on the details. Buried at Wortham, Texas.

NECK'S NOTES: He is the reason a man alone with a guitar got recorded at all. Before Lemon sold, the labels wanted a woman out front and a band behind her. He proved one voice and six strings could move records, and every scout in the South went looking for the next one. Listen to the time: he stretches a bar because the line isn't finished. That isn't sloppy playing — that's a singer who never had to fit a drummer.

PARAMOUNT · GRAFTON CHARLEY
PATTON c. 1891 – 1934 DOCKERY FARMS, MISS.

THE FATHER OF THE DELTA BLUES

CHARLEY PATTON

Born
About 1891, near Bolton, Mississippi. The family moved to Dockery Farms while he was a boy, and Dockery became the school.
Taught
Willie Brown, Tommy Johnson, Son House, Chester Burnett — who became Howlin' Wolf. Robert Johnson grew up downstream of all of it.
Recorded
First session June 1929, Richmond, Indiana, for Paramount. Some sides went out anonymously as "The Masked Marvel" — the label made a contest of guessing him.
Sides
Pony Blues · Down the Dirt Road Blues · A Spoonful Blues · High Water Everywhere, cut about the 1927 flood
Died
28 April 1934, Indianola, Mississippi, of a bad heart, two months after his last session. Buried at Holly Ridge.

NECK'S NOTES: The showman. Played the guitar behind his head and between his knees eighty years before anybody made a career of it, and beat on the body while he did it because he was one man doing the job of a rhythm section. That hoarse shout of his goes straight into Wolf and out the other side into rock and roll. If you only ever learn one thing off these records, learn that the guitar is a drum first.

WHITE STATION · MISS. HOWLIN'
WOLF 1910 – 1976 CHESTER A. BURNETT

PATTON'S STUDENT, IN PERSON

HOWLIN' WOLF

Born
Chester Arthur Burnett, 10 June 1910, White Station, near West Point, Mississippi. Named after a president. Grew to six foot three.
Learned
Guitar from Charley Patton at Dockery around 1930 — he could still play Patton's pieces note for note decades later. Harmonica from Rice Miller, the second Sonny Boy Williamson, who was family by marriage.
The howl
By his own account he was trying to yodel like Jimmie Rodgers, couldn't get it, and what came out instead was the howl.
Recorded
Memphis, 1951, for Sam Phillips — Moanin' at Midnight and How Many More Years. Two labels fought over the tapes. Chicago from 1952, and Chess after that.
Why he's here
He is the living wire. A man who learned at Dockery in 1930 was still on a stage in the seventies, so the pre-war Delta never actually ended — it moved.

NECK'S NOTES: People file Wolf under Chicago and stop there. Wrong end of the story. He was twenty years old standing in front of Charley Patton, and he carried that man's repertoire north in his hands. When you hear that band stomp on one chord and refuse to move — that's Dockery, plugged in.

THE ROPE: Lemon proves the solo bluesman sells (1926) → the scouts comb the South → Patton is found and recorded (1929) → Patton teaches Burnett at Dockery → Burnett becomes Wolf and takes it to Chicago (1952) → everything you have ever heard on a radio.

☞ NECK ☜

Neck plays pre-war blues. Only pre-war blues.

Not blues-rock, not a shuffle with a blues name on it, and nothing written after the shellac ran out in 1942. The Delta and the Texas country blues, played on the instruments those records were made on, at the tempos those men actually took.

The other half of it is what he knows. Ask him about a side and you get the label, the session, the town, who else was in the room, and which of the three surviving copies the reissue was mastered from. He has the kind of knowledge that usually comes with a university office attached, except he'd rather play it than footnote it.

NECK & THE HEADS is that repertoire with a band behind it.
